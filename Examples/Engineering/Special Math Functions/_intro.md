[CalcpadCE](https://calcpad-ce.org) builds [special functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_functions) from first principles: with `$Integral`, `$Sum`, `$Product` and a handful of standard transcendentals, the [gamma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_function), [beta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_function), [error](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Error_function), [Riemann zeta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_zeta_function), [Fresnel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fresnel_integral) and [elliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_integral) families — together with the [digamma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digamma_function) and [polygamma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygamma_function), [Dirichlet eta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirichlet_eta_function), [sine and cosine integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_integral), [logarithmic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithmic_integral_function) and [exponential integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_integral), [Dawson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dawson_function) and [Jacobi elliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_elliptic_functions) functions — are defined directly from their integral representations and called like any other function.

The [companion worksheet](#special-math-functions_1) walks through that construction end to end.
Each function is given by its defining integral or series, evaluated symbolically and plotted across a representative range, with checks against known identities (factorials, $\zeta(2) = \pi^2/6$, Apéry's constant, …) so you can verify the numerical accuracy at a glance.
The result is a transparent reference that doubles as a template for any further special function you might need.
